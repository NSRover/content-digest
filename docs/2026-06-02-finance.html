The current rebound in oil prices, keeping WTI in the $90-$105 "conflict range," significantly increases the risk of a global energy shock turning into a full-blown economic and monetary crisis, as the global economy was already fragile. Key points
  • Oil prices are currently trapped in a "conflict range" of roughly $90-$105 for WTI, indicating market indecision between a full-blown supply catastrophe and a clean resolution.
  • A sustained break below $90-$91 for WTI could signal either a positive supply normalization or a negative demand destruction, with the latter being indicated by falling front-end interest rates.
  • A sustained break above $107 for WTI would signal a dangerous intensification of the energy shock, driven by physical tightness, geopolitical fear, or forced buying.
  • The global economy is already weak, with many countries experiencing negative GDP, declining retail sales, and fragile labor markets, making it highly susceptible to prolonged high energy prices.
  • Elevated oil prices lead to a "dollar shock" as demand for dollars increases for energy imports while dollar supply becomes restricted, causing currency instability in countries like Japan, India, and Turkey.
  • Central banks' attempts to stabilize currencies through rate hikes and interventions are failing because the issue is a global dollar shortage, not just domestic policy rates.
