Why China’s Oil Demand May Be The Biggest Macro Signal Right Now
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

The global oil market has radically shifted into contango, marked by Saudi Arabia slashing its benchmark crude prices for Asia by $11 a barrel in its largest monthly reduction since at least 2000. This dramatic change, alongside historic discounts on crude grades tied to Chinese demand, signals a severe economic downturn in China as its domestic demand collapses and it ceases to act as the marginal buyer for global oil.

Key points
  • Saudi Arabia has aggressively cut its official selling price for Arab light crude to Asian buyers by $11 a barrel for August loading, putting it at a discount to the regional benchmark for the first time since 2020. This historic price cut, alongside even larger cuts of $15 a barrel for European buyers and $8 for US buyers, indicates that producers are struggling to find buyers as Chinese interest dries up.
  • The global oil futures market has flipped from backwardation into contango, meaning nearby oil is cheaper than oil for delivery later on. This shift creates an economic incentive to store oil rather than use it immediately, serving as a classic futures market signal for oversupply that historically clusters around major global economic downturns, such as in 2008, 2015, and 2020.
  • China's internal economy is experiencing a severe, accelerating retrenchment across multiple sectors. Retail sales fell year-over-year in May for the first annual decline since the 2022 lockdowns, auto sales collapsed by 16%, and real estate investment fell by 16% in the first five months of the year, showing that domestic demand is failing to support the economy.
  • A major structural shift is occurring in China's financial system as bank lending collapses and bond issuance rises to represent 30% of outstanding credit. Because bond issuance under stress redistributes existing savings rather than creating new broad money like bank lending does, this shift indicates a damaged private credit engine that is increasingly reliant on government borrowing.
  • Global financial indicators are aligning with the oil market's warning, as US Treasury TIPS breakeven rates have crashed, with the five-year breakeven rate dropping 40 basis points. This decline shows that markets are pricing in the disinflationary downside of weak global demand and demand destruction rather than a durable inflation shock.
Takeaway

For a European retail investor with a 50,000 euro portfolio, the strong macroeconomic signals of a Chinese economic slowdown and global disinflation suggest the following actionable considerations:

  • Defensive Long-Term Positioning: Given the sharp drop in TIPS breakeven rates and the emerging global disinflationary trend, long-term investors may want to increase exposure to high-quality government bonds (such as US Treasuries or European equivalents) which perform well when nominal growth and inflation expectations weaken.
  • Short-Term Trade Adjustments: Avoid or short equities and commodities highly sensitive to Chinese industrial demand, particularly oil and energy-related equities, as the physical oil market's shift into contango suggests continued downward pressure on prices.
  • Monitor Hong Kong and Emerging Markets: Keep a close eye on the Hang Seng index as a leading indicator; its ongoing weakness relative to US tech-driven markets confirms that smart money is actively pricing in China's credit and economic crisis.
  • Alternative Safe Havens: Consider allocating a portion of the portfolio to physical gold or yield-generating gold platforms to hedge against systemic financial risks stemming from China's banking and property crisis.

The Next "Money-Losing Bubble" is Here.
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

Howard Marks warns that the current AI IPO frenzy involving companies like SpaceX, Anthropic, and OpenAI resembles a speculative, money-losing bubble similar to the late 1990s dot-com era. Because these companies lack predictable long-term earnings, investing in them at current valuations is closer to speculation than analytical value investing.

Key points
  • Traditional value investing relies on analyzing a company's current earnings and forecasting its future earning power over 5 to 10 years to determine a fair price. This analytical approach is highly unfeasible with current AI IPOs because their future cash flows are virtually impossible to predict with any certainty.
  • Successful forecasting requires both a future projection and a high probability of that projection being correct. While AI is a transformative technology, the extreme uncertainty surrounding which specific companies will emerge as profitable winners makes highly confident long-term forecasts impossible.
  • The current AI boom mirrors historical technological bubbles, such as the railroads in the 1860s, radio in the 1920s, and the internet in 2000. In all these past cycles, excessive capital flowed in, too much infrastructure was built, valuations became overblown, and many investors ultimately lost their money when the market corrected.
  • Investors can navigate the AI landscape by choosing where to play on a risk spectrum. This spectrum ranges from lower-risk "hyperscalers" with established moats and diverse cash flows, to mid-risk pure-play AI companies like OpenAI or Nvidia, down to high-risk startups that function essentially as lottery tickets.
  • Investors do not have to participate in the tech sector to find value. Highly predictable, "boring" sectors with less intellectual content—such as energy, food, timber, home building, transportation, metals and mining, paper, and chemicals—are far less likely to be disrupted by AI and offer more stable valuation opportunities.
Takeaway

For a European retail investor with 50,000 euros, a dual long-term and short-term mindset, the actionable insights are:

  • For the short-term trade mindset: If participating in high-risk AI IPOs or pure-play startups (like Anthropic or OpenAI), treat these allocations strictly as speculative "lottery tickets." Limit these positions to a very small percentage of the 50,000 euro portfolio, recognizing that the probability of losing the capital is high despite the potential for incalculable upside.
  • For the long-term investing mindset: Focus the core of the portfolio on the lower-risk end of the AI spectrum, such as "hyperscalers" (large, diversified tech giants with massive operating cash flows and established moats) which are more resilient to market corrections.
  • For a conservative value approach: Avoid the AI hype entirely and allocate capital to predictable, unloved, and non-disruptive sectors. Look for opportunities in boring industries like energy, food, timber, home building, transportation, chemicals, or metals and mining, where cash flows are stable and easier to model using traditional discounted cash flow analysis.

Why Are Central Banks Buying So Much Gold? What Do They Know That We Don't?
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

Central banks have shifted from decades of selling gold to buying nearly 1,000 tons annually since 2022. This massive accumulation suggests that the institutions managing the fiat currency system are hedging against an unsustainable global debt explosion, weaponized reserve assets, and long-term inflation.

Key points
  • For decades, central banks were net sellers of gold, disposing of thousands of tons in the 1990s and 2000s under the belief that gold was an obsolete relic in a stable, globalized fiat system.
  • Since 2022, central banks have reversed this trend, purchasing nearly 1,000 tons of gold per year, driven by institutions from both emerging and developed markets including China, Poland, India, Turkey, and Singapore.
  • The explosion of global government, corporate, and private equity debt since 2008 and 2020 has made it clear to central bankers that debt cannot grow faster than GDP forever without causing severe inflation.
  • The freezing of Russia's foreign reserves in 2022 by the US and EU weaponized the global financial system, prompting other nations to seek gold as a neutral reserve asset that cannot be sanctioned or frozen.
  • Unlike government bonds or bank deposits, gold carries no counterparty or default risk, making it the ultimate neutral reserve asset for a potential transition toward a multipolar monetary system or a major monetary reset.
Takeaway

For a European retail investor with 50,000 euros, the massive buying of gold by central banks serves as a strong signal to align personal portfolios with the actions of monetary authorities.

  • Long-term investing mindset: Allocate a portion of the 50,000 euros to physical gold to act as "your own central bank." This provides a long-term hedge against currency debasement, systemic debt failure, and potential monetary resets, ensuring wealth preservation free of counterparty and sanction risks.
  • Short-term trading mindset: While short-term price fluctuations may cause concern, the underlying structural demand from global central banks establishes a strong fundamental floor for the gold market, making dips in the gold price potential buying opportunities rather than signs of a dying trend.
