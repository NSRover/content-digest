The Secret Mechanics behind the Debasement Trade Unwind...

TLDR

The long-term debasement trade of hard assets outperforming fiat currency remains valid, but it does not play out in a straight line due to the mechanics of the global dollar system. When credit contractions or geopolitical crises occur, a scramble for dollar liquidity forces the selling of liquid reserve assets like gold and silver, driving the US dollar higher and causing brutal drawdowns in commodities.

Key points
  • The long-term debasement thesis is correct because fiat currencies naturally lose purchasing power over time, meaning hard assets and commodities will inevitably go higher over decades. However, the path is highly volatile and characterized by severe cyclical drawdowns rather than a straight upward trajectory.
  • Credit contractions and the unwinding of global carry trades temporarily derail the debasement trade. Because the global financial system runs on debt-based dollar liabilities, a squeeze in dollar liquidity forces market participants to liquidate their most liquid hard assets to obtain the dollars required to settle obligations.
  • Gold acting as a central bank reserve asset explains why it gets sold during geopolitical crises, such as the Iran conflict. When liquidity dries up and global transaction costs rise, central banks and institutions sell their appreciated gold reserves to acquire the dollars necessary to purchase essential commodities and service debts.
  • The global economy operates on two competing carry trades: the global US dollar carry trade and local currency carry trades. Fluctuations in the dollar index determine which of these carry trades is actively working against foreign nations, creating a specific trading band outside of which severe market unwinds occur.
  • A rising dollar index paradoxically drives de-dollarization by putting immense pressure on foreign economies, leading to defaults and debt destruction. Conversely, a falling dollar index leads to "re-dollarization" as the rest of the world takes on even more dollar-denominated debt due to cheaper borrowing costs.
Takeaway
  • For long-term portfolios, maintain core allocations to hard assets like gold and silver, but avoid using leverage or going "all in" during peak market enthusiasm, as drawdowns can reach 25% to 40%.
  • For short-term trading, monitor the US Dollar Index (DXY) as a liquidity weather vane; a breaking out or rising DXY above key moving averages (50, 100, 200-day) serves as a warning signal to reduce exposure to commodities and precious metals.
  • Utilize a dollar-cost averaging strategy for purchasing commodities and precious metals to mitigate the risk of buying at cyclical peaks, ensuring you have secondary sources of liquidity so you are never forced to liquidate assets during a market squeeze.
