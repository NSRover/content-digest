How to Invest with a Full Time Job? (& save LIFE-TIME commissions)!
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)fallback
TLDR

Retail investors with full-time jobs can manage their own investments and potentially save significant commissions by adopting simple, structured strategies. Key approaches include systematic investing in better-performing global indexes, using a core-satellite portfolio allocation, and employing rule-based profit-taking. These methods aim to empower individuals to invest effectively without relying on active fund managers.

Key points
  • Actively managed mutual funds often fail to beat their benchmark indexes, and the commissions charged can significantly erode long-term returns, potentially reducing a portfolio's value by a third over decades.
  • Investors should consider systematic investment plans (SIPs) in global index funds, as historical data suggests indexes like QQQ and S&P 500 have outperformed Indian indexes such as Nifty50 and midcap indexes over various timeframes.
  • A "core and satellite" portfolio strategy involves allocating 80-85% to stable, high-growth "core" companies (e.g., Meta, Microsoft, Google) and 15-20% to higher-risk "satellite" picks (e.g., Rocket Lab), with satellite allocations never exceeding 2.5% per stock.
  • The "coffee can" investing approach, suitable for long-term holding, should focus on businesses demonstrating consistent revenue and profit margin growth, along with high survivability and low disruption risk, rather than just well-known brands.
  • Channel trading, which involves buying at support levels and selling at resistance levels within a stock's established trend, can be effective for well-studied, high-quality stocks and can be managed with minimal weekly monitoring.
  • Implementing rule-based position management, such as taking 20-40% profits when a stock hits a target or achieves 100% gains, is crucial for realizing returns and should be a standard practice, contrary to the "never book profits" advice often associated with mutual funds.
  • A barbell strategy balances high-risk, high-reward investments (e.g., Bitcoin, speculative tech stocks) with stable, low-risk instruments (e.g., gold, value stocks) to manage overall portfolio risk and growth potential.

How Overvalued is the Stock Market, Really?
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)fallback
TLDR

The stock market is currently expensive by most historical measures, with the S&P 500 PE ratio, Shiller PE, and Buffett Indicator all flashing warning signs. However, unlike the dot-com bubble, the "Magnificent Seven" companies driving much of the market's returns have strong earnings growth, diversified business models, and robust balance sheets, making a major crash unlikely without an unforeseen black swan event.

Key points
  • The S&P 500's current PE ratio of 32 is historically high, comparable to the dot-com bubble when excluding periods of earnings collapse. This suggests a market where investors are paying a premium for future growth expectations.
  • While the "Magnificent Seven" companies (Apple, Amazon, Meta, Google, Nvidia, Microsoft, Tesla) have significantly contributed to S&P 500 returns, their individual PE ratios (excluding Tesla's outlier) are not as extreme as those seen during the dot-com bubble for leading tech companies.
  • A key difference from the dot-com era is that the Magnificent Seven companies are currently experiencing strong earnings growth and possess diversified business models, meaning their valuations are not solely dependent on the success of a single emerging technology like AI.
  • The Shiller PE ratio, which uses a 10-year average of inflation-adjusted earnings, is at 42, nearly matching its all-time high during the tech bubble, indicating a very frothy market from a long-term cyclical perspective.
  • The Buffett Indicator (Wilshire GDP ratio), comparing total market cap to US GDP, stands at approximately 210%, the highest in history, suggesting the market is significantly overvalued relative to the economy's output, though this is somewhat skewed by global profits of US companies.
  • Despite high valuations, a major market crash is considered unlikely without an unexpected black swan event, as the leading companies have strong cash flows and solid balance sheets, distinguishing them from the pre-revenue speculative companies of the dot-com bubble.

  • China is actively encouraging its citizens to buy physical gold by lowering transaction costs on bank accumulation accounts, exempting these transactions from taxes, and allowing domestic insurers to hold gold assets.
  • The historical gold backing of the US dollar is currently around 20%, well below its 100-year average of 45.5%, signaling a potential supply squeeze as foreign central banks seek to replace fiat reserves with physical gold.
  • Geopolitical instability, including rising conflict in the Middle East affecting shipping routes and oil prices, is driving up energy costs and further incentivizing nations to hold gold as a non-freezable, universally accepted reserve asset.
