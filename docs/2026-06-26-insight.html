How Should We Regulate AI?
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)
TLDR

The discussion around AI regulation is too focused on preventing harms and needs to shift towards defining a public agenda for what we want AI to achieve, especially in the public sector.

Key points
  • For AI to solve a problem, there needs to be financial backing due to the high computational cost, and the problem must be "legible" to the system, meaning data needs to be created for machine learning to work on.
  • The public sector often lacks the necessary data creation and financial investment to leverage AI effectively for public good.
  • A practical application of AI in government could be an IRS-developed LLM that helps individuals with their taxes, as the IRS has access to income data and the tax code.
  • More broadly, AI could act as a concierge for government services, helping citizens navigate complex systems by understanding their situation and what the government can offer.
  • While preventing AI harms and addressing existential risk are important, there is a critical need for a "theory of AI goods" to guide its development and application for societal benefit.
Takeaway

Instead of solely focusing on what we don't want AI to do, we should actively define what we *do* want it to achieve, particularly in the public sector. This involves recognizing that AI solutions require financial investment and "legible" data, which often needs to be intentionally created. By shifting our mindset to a "theory of AI goods," we can identify opportunities for AI to simplify complex systems, like government services, and empower individuals to navigate them more effectively, rather than just reacting to potential negative outcomes.

Why Nobody Feels Loved Anymore - Sonja Lyubomirsky
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)
Guest

Sonja Lyubomirsky is a psychologist, professor, and author who pioneered happiness interventions in 1998. She has been studying happiness for 36 years and is the co-author of the book "How to Feel Loved."

TLDR

The key to feeling loved and achieving happiness lies in fostering genuine connection with others through specific mindsets and communication skills, rather than solely focusing on being "lovable" or changing external circumstances.

Key points
  • The most effective happiness interventions share a commonality: they make individuals feel more connected to and loved by others. This suggests that feeling connected is fundamental to human well-being and survival.
  • Trying to be "lovable" by broadcasting positive qualities or achieving external markers like wealth or fame is a myth that does not lead to feeling truly loved; instead, it often leads to admiration, not genuine connection.
  • The key to feeling loved is being known, which requires sharing more of oneself, including vulnerabilities and genuine opinions, rather than just a highlight reel. This process should be gradual and responsive to the other person's curiosity and receptiveness.
  • Receiving love is a skill that can be developed with practice, similar to accepting compliments. Many people struggle to internalize love, even when it is present, due to factors like attachment styles or low self-esteem.
  • Genuine curiosity and active listening are crucial habits for fostering connection and making others feel loved. Asking deep questions and truly hearing responses, rather than rehearsing one's own reply or offering unsolicited advice, is vital.
  • The "multiplicity" mindset involves seeing others in their full complexity, acknowledging both positive and negative qualities without judgment or minimization. This fosters compassion and deeper understanding in relationships.
  • How individuals respond to good news in a relationship is a stronger predictor of relationship duration than how they respond to bad news. Truly celebrating a partner's successes, even if they trigger personal insecurities, is essential for relationship strength.
Takeaway

To cultivate a deeper sense of being loved and to navigate relationships more effectively, focus on actively practicing curiosity, listening, and sharing. Instead of trying to be perfect or constantly "lovable," dare to be known by revealing your authentic self gradually and observing how others respond. Understand that feeling loved is not just about receiving affection, but about believing you matter in someone's life, which is built through genuine engagement and mutual understanding. Prioritize relationships and develop social skills, recognizing that these are not innate but can be learned and improved over time, much like any other skill. When faced with discomfort or vulnerability, take small steps and remember that showing your human side often makes you more relatable and liked, not less.
