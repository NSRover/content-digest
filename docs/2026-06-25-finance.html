Key points
  • Gold and silver are undergoing liquidations, with gold down 26% from its January peak and silver down 50%, driven by a strengthening dollar and its role as a reserve asset during dollar shortages.
  • The dollar is spiking, not due to interest rate differentials or central bank policies, but because of a mechanical relationship with global dollar funding conditions and a shortage of dollars in the eurodollar system.
  • Foreign governments and central banks are selling reserve assets, including US Treasuries and gold, to raise liquid dollar balances, which mechanically correlates with a rising dollar exchange value.
  • TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities) break-even rates are crashing, indicating that the market never truly priced in significant inflation risk and is now signaling demand destruction and deflationary expectations beyond just oil price normalization.
  • The 2-year/10-year Treasury yield curve is flattening and nearing reinversion, signifying the market's strong disagreement with the Federal Reserve's policy and its expectation of future rate cuts due to underlying economic weakness.
  • Silver is expected to fall further, with the gold-to-silver ratio potentially returning to 80 or 90, as there is insufficient industrial demand to support higher silver prices, especially with a struggling global economy.

  • Gold has corrected by roughly 25% because the central bank diversification trade that drove its initial rise has largely played out. It remains a useful wealth preservation tool and portfolio hedge at a 5% to 7% allocation, but it is a non-productive asset likely to move sideways or return a modest 8% to 10% over the long term.
  • Silver is a structurally broken asset that has fallen 50% due to declining industrial and consumer demand, making it a poor investment compared to gold, which remains the apex commodity. Commodity investing carries high concentration risk because investors cannot easily diversify across 20 different commodities the way they can with equities.
  • Meta and HDFC Bank are examples of broken prices rather than broken businesses, as both maintain strong fundamentals, growing operating profits, and intact moats despite their stock price corrections. Meta is highlighted as a high-conviction buy due to its accelerating revenue and growing operating cash flows.
  • Bitcoin is a highly volatile, speculative asset whose core thesis as non-government money remains intact alongside growing international regulation. Investors should expect 50% drawdowns if they want 100% run-ups, manage risk through strict position sizing (capping allocation at 5%), and accumulate only within designated price zones.
  • Indian equities have experienced a 15% to 20% net negative return over the last two years when adjusted for INR depreciation, facing structural drags like Chinese dumping and a lack of independent foreign policy. While the Indian economy is not structurally broken, the market is more of a trading play than a hyper-growth investment play.
  • Indian IT giant TCS has suffered a massive price-to-earnings (PE) multiple contraction from 40x down to 15x despite maintaining record-high revenues and profits. This represents a mix of a broken price and a partially challenged business model, making it suitable for gradual position building rather than going overweight.
Takeaway
  • For the Long-Term Mindset: Focus your capital on high-quality global equities where the price is broken but the business is intact (such as Meta). Limit your exposure to speculative assets like Bitcoin to a maximum of 5% of your portfolio, and accumulate it only in predefined price zones without downward averaging once your allocation cap is met. Maintain a 5% to 7% hedge in gold for wealth preservation, but avoid silver entirely.
  • For the Short-Term Trade Mindset: Approach Indian equities, particularly Indian IT (like TCS) and banking (like HDFC), as trading plays rather than long-term hyper-growth investments. Build half-positions in these beaten-down sectors to capture potential valuation reratings (PE expansion) once market clarity returns, but be prepared to cut losses if your investment thesis breaks.

  • The US Mint is releasing a highly limited run of only 2,026 Liberty Bell gold coins weighing one troy ounce and priced at $19,600, which is likely a collector's item rather than a signal of an impending official government revaluation. A corresponding silver version is priced at $750 for a half-ounce.
  • The statutory price of gold held by the US Treasury has remained unchanged at $42.22 per ounce since 1973, whereas other global central banks mark their gold reserves to market prices.
  • Revaluing the official gold reserves to market prices would allow the US Treasury to generate up to a trillion dollars in cash from the Federal Reserve without issuing new debt, a mechanism discussed in a Federal Reserve Board of Governors paper.
  • Economist Judy Shelton has proposed that the US announce a gold-backed "trust bond" on July 4th to celebrate the nation's 250th anniversary, which some speculate could be paired with a gold revaluation to support larger bond issuances.
  • Macroeconomic indicators remain highly favorable for precious metals, with the US national debt approaching $40 trillion, the annual deficit running at $2 trillion, M2 money supply growing at nearly 6% year-over-year, and the Federal Reserve's balance sheet quietly expanding by $200 billion since December 2025.
Takeaway
  • For long-term investing: Maintain or increase allocations to physical gold, physical silver, and precious metals mining stocks as a hedge against ongoing fiat currency debasement, rising national debt, and covert quantitative easing.
  • For short-term trading: Avoid high-premium collector coins like the $19,600 Liberty Bell gold coin unless you are strictly a numismatic collector, as the steep premium does not reflect the intrinsic value of the underlying one ounce of gold.
