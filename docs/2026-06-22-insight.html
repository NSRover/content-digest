How To Find Your Calling
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)
TLDR

Your calling is the thing you can't stop thinking about, not necessarily the most fun or messianic pursuit, and it's fundamentally about creating real value that others need and recognize.

Key points
  • A calling is often something that persistently occupies your thoughts because you find it deeply interesting, rather than something you consciously decide will make you a savior or is simply the most enjoyable activity.
  • The pursuit of a calling isn't always "fun"; it's frequently something you feel compelled to do, driven by an internal necessity.
  • The core objective of a calling is to create genuine value with your life, earning success through tangible contributions.
  • True success in a calling comes from being recognized and acknowledged for the real value you create, not from superficial means like flattery or undeserved kindness.
  • A key indicator of a true calling is whether your efforts result in something that genuinely serves a need for others.
Takeaway

Instead of searching for a "fun" or "heroic" path, pay attention to what you genuinely can't stop thinking about – that persistent interest is a strong signal. Reframe success not as personal enjoyment or external validation, but as the creation of real, needed value that earns genuine recognition. This shift in perspective can help you identify and pursue work that is deeply meaningful and impactful.

The Vicious Cost of Self-Awareness
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)
TLDR

Self-awareness, while generally seen as a positive trait, can paradoxically lead to inaction and paralysis because our minds vividly simulate negative outcomes, making us avoid potential failures even at the cost of missed opportunities and prolonged suffering.

Key points
  • The phrase "Thus conscience does make cowards of us all" from Hamlet suggests that self-awareness, or consciousness, can inhibit action by allowing us to imagine and emotionally experience negative consequences before they occur.
  • Our ability to simulate worst-case scenarios makes us treat them as if they are already real, leading to hesitation and avoidance, where inaction feels like safety.
  • Reflection can drain our will because it multiplies potential outcomes faster than our actions can address them, causing us to linger and feel as if we've already failed before even trying.
  • We tend to learn quickly from mistakes we make (commission errors) but rarely feel the cost of mistakes we avoid (omission errors), which explains why people stay in undesirable situations for years.
  • Our minds prefer familiar misery over the uncertainty of unfamiliar freedom, even enduring long-term suffering to avoid short-term pain or the unknown.
  • Beyond a certain point, self-awareness can inhibit agency; less reflection and less certainty can sometimes lead to more movement and a more lived life.
Takeaway

To overcome the paralysis of overthinking, recognize that your mind's ability to simulate negative outcomes can be a greater barrier than actual pain or effort. Actively confront "omission errors" – the costs of things you *don't* do – by consciously bringing them into awareness, as they are often hidden. Consider front-loading the pain of inaction by vividly imagining the long-term costs of not pursuing a desired change, and conversely, the benefits of taking action, to motivate yourself to move forward despite uncertainty.
