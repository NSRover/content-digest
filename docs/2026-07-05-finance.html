Private Credit Just Burst The $25 Trillion AI Bubble
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

The private credit market is entering a defensive phase marked by massive, repeated redemption requests and trapped capital, which is beginning to deflate the capital-intensive AI data center bubble. As shadow banks restrict withdrawals and tighten lending standards, major developers are abandoning mega-projects and selling off stakes, shifting the AI narrative from endless growth to strict downside underwriting.

Key points
  • Blue Owl has faced massive, consecutive quarterly redemption requests, with its largest private credit fund hit by withdrawal requests for 18.8% of its shares and its smaller technology-focused partner hit by requests for 38.1% of its shares in the second quarter.
  • Private credit funds are currently keeping roughly $14 billion trapped through quarterly withdrawal caps and gates, transitioning these vehicles from growth engines into defensive liquidity management machines focused on preserving cash.
  • The industry's defensive shift is signaled by operational changes, including leadership exits at a troubled BlackRock private credit fund and a global reassurance roadshow by Blue Owl executives who previously dismissed critic concerns.
  • The financing squeeze is directly impacting the AI infrastructure boom, as evidenced by Blackstone selling its stakes in three Northern Virginia data centers to Digital Realty for $3.5 billion.
  • Major developers backed by private capital are abandoning massive projects due to changing financial calculations, highlighted by QTS terminating its portion of the $100 billion Digital Gateway data center campus in Virginia, following a similar exit by Brookfield-backed Compass Data Centers.
  • The AI bubble is highly vulnerable to these financing shifts because data centers are capital-intensive physical assets that rely on easy, elastic debt rather than just software success, meaning the bubble can deflate simply because the marginal dollar becomes more skeptical.
Takeaway

For a retail European investor with a €50,000 portfolio, the key actionable insights are:

  • Re-evaluate AI and Tech Exposure: Recognize that the AI boom is heavily dependent on easy debt financing from shadow banks. A long-term investor should scrutinize holdings in AI infrastructure, data center developers, and highly leveraged tech firms, as their valuations may decline if financing costs rise and capital becomes more selective.
  • Exercise Caution with Private Credit and Illiquid Yield Products: Avoid committing new capital to private credit funds or similar illiquid alternative debt vehicles. Existing investors in these structures are facing strict withdrawal gates, meaning your capital could easily become trapped during a market downturn.
  • Focus on Cash Flow and Tangible Assets: For both short-term trades and long-term positions, pivot toward high-quality companies with strong organic cash flows, secured power access, and signed leases, rather than speculative projects relying on future demand extrapolations.
