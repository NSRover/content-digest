TLDR

HSBC and other major banks are quietly pulling back from funding private credit funds by refusing to renew credit lines, cutting back leverage, and marking down collateral. This insider de-risking contrasts sharply with public junk bond markets, where retail and passive flows keep credit spreads historically tight and complacent.

Key points
  • HSBC is retreating from riskier private credit lending by refusing to renew credit facilities and pulling back leverage from risky funds due to concerns over collateral and underwriting standards.
  • Private credit funds rely heavily on banks for "back leverage" (borrowing against their own loan portfolios) to amplify returns, meaning bank pullbacks directly threaten the funds' economics and liquidity.
  • Other major banks, including JP Morgan, Goldman Sachs, and Barclays, are also raising rates on leverage and marking down individual loans posted as collateral, forcing fund managers to swap out assets.
  • Public high-yield credit spreads remain historically tight, creating a "split screen" where retail and passive investment flows keep public markets calm while banking insiders quietly exit.
  • The divergence occurs because passive retirement flows mechanically allocate money into junk bonds as the stock market rises, creating a false signal of safety that ignores deteriorating fundamental credit conditions.
Takeaway
  • For the long-term mindset: Avoid reaching for yield in high-yield junk bonds or retail private credit funds, as current tight spreads do not compensate for the underlying credit and collateral risks being flagged by major banks.
  • For the short-term trade mindset: Monitor the widening gap between insider bank behavior and public credit spreads; a sudden shift in public market liquidity could present short opportunities in high-yield credit ETFs or exposed financial sector assets.

Takeaway
  • For long-term investing: Consider allocating a portion of your portfolio to physical gold and silver held outside the banking system as a hedge against systemic risk and the potential collapse of the fiat dollar-based monetary system.
  • For short-term trading: Monitor the divergence between retail sentiment (selling gold ETFs) and central bank actions (buying physical gold) to identify potential buying opportunities during price corrections.
