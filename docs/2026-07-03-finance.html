  • The headline establishment survey showed payrolls rising by a weak 57,000 in June, missing expectations, while May's figures were revised down sharply from 172,000 to 129,000. This pattern of downward revisions mirrors previous years and suggests the June figure may eventually be revised into negative territory.
  • The household survey revealed a catastrophic decline, with employment plunging by 507,000 in June alone and down 833,000 since January. Full-time employment also fell by half a million in June, threatening consumer spending, business revenues, and overall economic confidence.
  • The official unemployment rate's slight decline to 4.2% is highly misleading because it was driven by a massive collapse in the labor force, which plummeted by 720,000 in June and is down 1.1 million since January. When accounting for these labor force dropouts, the adjusted unofficial unemployment rate rises to a new high of 6.1%.

This temporary activity borrowed demand from the future, and that future has now arrived.
  • Multiple forward-looking indicators corroborate this labor market deterioration, including plunging Treasury inflation-protected securities (TIPS) break-even rates pointing to demand destruction, NFIB small business data showing hiring plans hitting their lowest levels since May 2020, and a sharp drop in the Conference Board's measure of CEO confidence.

Japan's decades-long role as the world's cheapest source of funding is ending as JGB yields surge and the yen weakens sharply, threatening to withdraw liquidity from global markets.

Key points
  • Japan supplied massive liquidity to the world for decades through near-zero and negative interest rates, enabling investors and institutions everywhere to borrow cheaply in yen and invest in US Treasuries, equities, real estate, and other assets.
  • The Bank of Japan has raised its base rate to 1 percent for the first time since the mid-1990s, yet longer-term JGB yields have climbed far above that level, with the 30-year yield exceeding 4 percent and the 10-year near 2.7 percent.
  • Despite higher policy rates, the yen continues to weaken, trading at a 40-year low against the dollar above 162, because rising long-term yields signal eroding confidence in Japan's fiscal position and the central bank's ability to control the curve.
  • Japan's government debt-to-GDP ratio near 230 percent, heavy energy import dependence, large refinancing needs, and reduced Bank of Japan bond purchases are driving capital outflows and making it harder to defend the currency.
  • If Japanese investors shift back into domestic bonds, they will likely sell foreign holdings, reversing decades of capital exports and removing a key source of liquidity from US, UK, and European bond and equity markets.
  • Government bonds, long viewed as risk-free after a 40-year bull market ending in 2021, are becoming riskier assets, while gold is highlighted for having no counterparty or default risk.
Takeaway

A European retail investor should monitor JGB yield movements closely as a leading indicator of global liquidity conditions. With a long-term horizon, gradually increasing exposure to gold or gold-linked instruments can serve as a hedge against rising sovereign bond risk and eroding confidence in fiat debt markets. For shorter-term positioning, watch for sharp yen rebounds or sudden JGB yield spikes that could trigger broader equity and bond market volatility, offering potential tactical entry or exit points.
