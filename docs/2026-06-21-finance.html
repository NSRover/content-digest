Something VERY Strange is Happening Just Happened Global Markets
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)
TLDR

While US tech and semiconductor stocks are surging, the Hong Kong Hang Seng index is falling, signaling a deeper problem in China's credit markets where bond financing is replacing bank lending due to weak demand and rising bad consumer debt, indicating a transition to "depression economics" similar to the post-2008 period in the US and Europe.

Key points
  • The Hong Kong Hang Seng index is significantly underperforming global markets, which are experiencing euphoria in tech and semiconductors. This divergence is critical because Hong Kong is a major financial gateway for China and the rest of Asia, reflecting global risk appetite and dollar funding conditions.
  • China's credit market is undergoing a significant shift, with bond financing now surpassing bank lending for new credit. While officially presented as a modernization towards a more efficient economy, this shift is actually a response to weak loan demand, risk-averse banks, and a collapsing property model.
  • The rise of bond financing in China is not a sign of strength but rather an emergency backstop, as bank lending, which creates new money and purchasing power, is retreating. Bond issuance primarily redistributes existing money, unlike bank loans which expand the money supply.
  • China is facing a slow-motion credit crisis, not just in property development but also in household debt, with estimates suggesting a significant portion of the adult population is behind on debt payments. This makes banks unwilling to extend new loans, leading them to buy low-yielding government bonds for safety.
  • The increasing reliance on government bond issuance to fill the credit gap is a sign of desperation and Keynesian economics, where the public sector steps in because the private sector is not borrowing or taking enough risk. This pattern is characteristic of "depression economics."
  • This situation in China mirrors the "silent depression" experienced in the US and Europe after 2008, where banks retreated, bond markets became a partial backstop, and economies entered a long period of slow growth and weak productivity. China's economy, being even more bank-dependent, faces potentially more severe consequences.
Takeaway

For a retail European investor with 50,000 euros, the video suggests a cautious approach, especially regarding investments tied to the Chinese economy. The divergence of the Hang Seng index from global tech euphoria, coupled with the underlying credit issues in China, indicates significant risk.

For a long-term investor, it would be prudent to reconsider or reduce exposure to Chinese assets, particularly those reliant on traditional bank lending or the property sector. The shift to government-led bond financing is not a sign of healthy growth but rather a symptom of deeper economic problems, which could lead to a prolonged period of slow growth. Diversifying away from heavily bank-dependent economies and towards markets with more organic credit creation might be a wise move.

For a short-term trader, the video highlights a potential contrarian signal. While US tech and semiconductors are "going vertical," the Hang Seng's decline suggests a fundamental weakness that could eventually impact global markets. This divergence could present opportunities for short positions in Chinese-related assets or long positions in assets that are less exposed to China's credit issues, if one believes the Hang Seng is a leading indicator of broader economic trouble. However, the video also cautions that no stock index is a perfect forecasting tool, so any short-term trades should be approached with careful risk management.
