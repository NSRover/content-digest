Guest

Steve Stewart-Williams is an evolutionary psychologist, professor, and author. His work focuses on the evolutionary origins of human behavior, including the biological and psychological differences between the sexes.

TLDR

While men and women are remarkably similar in general intelligence and core values, consistent biological differences—driven by evolutionary pressures like parental investment and reproductive variance—shape distinct average tendencies in personality, interests, and mating strategies. Acknowledging these differences is not a justification for discrimination, but rather a necessary step for accurate medical diagnosis, self-understanding, and avoiding the pitfalls of rigid social engineering.

Key points
  • The scientific definition of sex is based on anisogamy, the existence of two distinct gamete sizes (large eggs and small sperm), which creates a consistent binary across most sexually reproducing species.
  • Evolutionary psychology posits that many sex differences, such as the "people vs. things" interest divide or differences in aggression, are not merely social constructs but have innate components that persist across cultures and time.
  • The "gender equality paradox" reveals that in more egalitarian societies, sex differences in personality and career preferences often widen rather than narrow, suggesting that when social constraints are removed, individuals are more likely to gravitate toward their evolved predispositions.
  • Human reproductive strategies are unique among mammals because of high levels of biparental care, which reduces the reproductive skew (variance) between men and women, leading to more moderate sex differences in aggression and parenting than are seen in other mammals.
  • Men and women share highly similar core mate preferences—prioritizing kindness, intelligence, and stability—but differ in the weight placed on secondary traits like physical attractiveness (men) and resource/status indicators (women), which serve as evolutionary signals for fertility and investment capacity.
  • Denying or exaggerating sex differences carries real-world costs, such as the failure to recognize sex-specific medical symptoms (e.g., heart attacks in women or depression in men) and the implementation of social policies that ignore the reality of individual variation.
Takeaway

To navigate the world more effectively, recognize that "analysis is not justification." Understanding the evolutionary roots of behavior—such as the male tendency toward risk-taking or the female tendency toward higher agreeableness—allows you to depersonalize conflicts and make more informed choices. Rather than forcing yourself or others into "unisex" molds, aim for a "let people be themselves" approach that respects individual variation while acknowledging that biological averages exist. Use this knowledge to advocate for better medical care, recognize your own biases in mate selection, and avoid the trap of blaming individuals for outcomes that are influenced by deep-seated evolutionary pressures.
