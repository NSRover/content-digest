Japan Declared War on the Yen Carry Trade… It Won’t End Well
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

The Japanese government has finally recognized that the true yen carry trade is driven by domestic institutional capital—such as pension funds and insurance companies—fleeing Japan's weak economy for better risk-adjusted returns abroad. Mainstream economic theories and Bank of Japan rate hikes have backfired, destabilizing the domestic bond market and making Japan even less attractive to local investors. Without genuine economic growth and improved risk-adjusted returns, official efforts to reverse this capital flight and strengthen the yen are highly unlikely to succeed.

Key points
  • The mainstream narrative that the yen carry trade is driven by foreign hedge funds borrowing cheap yen is a cartoonish oversimplification. The real, much larger carry trade consists of massive Japanese domestic institutions, like pension funds and insurance companies, moving their capital out of Japan to seek better risk-adjusted returns overseas.
  • Decades of quantitative easing and fiscal stimulus have failed to generate organic economic growth in Japan. Consequently, domestic institutions avoid investing locally because of low growth, low inflation expectations, and a persistent lack of attractive investment opportunities.
  • Contrary to textbook economics, the Bank of Japan's interest rate hikes actually weaken the yen. Raising rates inorganically creates instability and uncertainty in the Japanese Government Bond (JGB) market, prompting domestic financial institutions to engage in a buyer strike and send even more capital abroad.
  • The only times the yen has significantly strengthened recently is when the carry trade reversed due to external factors, such as in July and August of 2024 when fears over the U.S. labor market and asset illiquidity caused Japanese investors to temporarily pull back their foreign investments.
  • Japanese policymakers remain trapped in a textbook economic framework that prevents them from solving the root issue. To naturally reverse the capital flight, Japan must become an attractive place to invest, which requires real economic growth and genuine opportunity—something the government and central bank do not know how to generate.

Michael Burry has revealed a significant new investment in Lululemon, viewing the beaten-down retail stock as a potential long-term turnaround play. Key points
  • Lululemon is currently facing five major headwinds: slowing sales growth (particularly in North America), margin pressure from US tariffs, self-inflicted product and inventory planning blunders, rising competition from brands like Alo and Vuori, and leadership uncertainty under interim co-CEOs.

  • Burry believes Lululemon's brand moat is still strong, noting that at least 95% of its customers do not shop at upstart competitors, and points out that the men's business has quietly grown into a multi-billion dollar segment.
  • Financially, the company remains highly profitable, generating $11 billion in annual revenue, $1.1 billion in cash over the last 12 months, and holding $1.5 billion in cash with virtually no debt, allowing management to aggressively buy back shares at a price-to-earnings ratio of 9.
  • Using his proprietary valuation framework, Burry classifies Lululemon as an "IV18," meaning he projects an 18% annual return over the next 15 years, and has backed this thesis by lowering his average purchase price to approximately $125 per share.
  • The success of the investment heavily relies on incoming CEO Heidi O'Neal, formerly of Nike, stabilizing operations, improving inventory management, and executing better product launches to prove the market's pessimism is misplaced.

The Biggest Myth in Finance: "The Debasement Trade Is Over".
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

Mainstream financial arguments claiming the "debasement trade" is over due to a new Federal Reserve chairman are incorrect because they ignore the inherent nature of fiat monetary systems. When faced with high debt levels, governments and central banks historically choose currency debasement over default, fiscal austerity, or higher taxes. Because real gold and silver cannot be created out of thin air and their production grows slowly, they remain the ultimate long-term stores of purchasing power.

Key points
  • Mainstream financial media claims that the debasement trade is ending under new Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh, but this view ignores over 300 years of monetary history showing that no fiat currency has ever maintained its purchasing power.
  • Fiat currency has no natural supply limit and is created by decree, central banks, and commercial bank credit, which inevitably dilutes the purchasing power of existing currency holders.
  • When debt reaches unsustainable levels, governments have four choices: default, fiscal austerity, higher taxes, or currency debasement. Historically, they almost always choose debasement because inflation acts as a politically easier, hidden tax.
  • Historical examples of currency debasement span from the silver content reduction of the Roman denarius to the paper currency collapses in revolutionary France, Weimar Germany, Argentina, and Zimbabwe.
  • Gold and silver preserve purchasing power over centuries because their physical supply cannot be artificially expanded; global gold production grows at only 1% to 2% annually, which closely aligns with long-term economic growth.
