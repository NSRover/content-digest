The sheer scale of AI-related debt and resource consumption threatens to crowd out the rest of the struggling global economy by absorbing finite capital, electricity, and industrial capacity.

Key points
  • Alphabet's capital expenditures doubled to nearly $45 billion in Q2 2026, resulting in a negative free cash flow of $5.9 billion and forcing the company to suspend share repurchases and raise $20 billion in debt and $85 billion in equity. JP Morgan estimates Google's capital expenditures could reach $378 billion in 2027, an amount comparable to the annual economic output of a medium-sized country.
  • The AI boom is creating a "price illusion" where nominal export values look spectacular but physical volumes remain flat. For example, Japan's export values rose 19.3% year-over-year while physical export volumes grew by only 0.2%, meaning higher spending is merely paying for inflated input costs rather than producing more physical infrastructure or computing capacity.
  • Thailand and China showcase a similar disconnect where AI-related export values are surging on paper, but domestic manufacturing volumes and broader economic indicators remain weak.   • AI infrastructure spending is no longer funded by internal corporate cash flows and has shifted heavily to debt markets, with AI-related debt issuance reaching $270 billion by early July 2026. This massive issuance is beginning to strain market absorption, as seen when Amazon had to offer higher yields and experienced weaker-than-average demand for its recent $25 billion bond sale.
  • The massive scale of AI investment is actively crowding out the rest of the economy by driving up the costs of finite resources like electricity, specialized construction, land, and electrical equipment. Furthermore, because data centers require very few permanent jobs to operate once built, this massive capital allocation fails to generate the employment or productivity gains historically associated with industrial revolutions.

This suggests the recent correction might be a temporary rotation rather than a sign of an impending market crash, unlike the dot-com bubble where earnings also started to roll over.
  • A major catalyst for changing earnings trajectories, even for strong companies, is an economic recession, which causes consumers and businesses to cut spending. Historically, recessions have led to market leaders' profits stalling or contracting, causing capital to flow out and market concentration to unwind.
  • The tightness of credit, measured by the percentage of banks tightening lending standards, is a reliable indicator of recession probability. Currently, only 8% of banks are tightening lending standards, down from 40% in 2023, indicating loose credit conditions that support corporate earnings and reinforce market concentration.
  • As long as credit remains loose and the economy avoids a recession, corporate earnings are likely to stay resilient, making the recent tech stock weakness a potential buying opportunity.
