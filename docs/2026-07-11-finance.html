THE WORLD IS RUNNING OUT OF DIESEL — Could This Crash the Global Economy? Peter Carlin.
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

The global economy is facing a severe shortage of diesel and jet fuel because refinery systems in the US and Europe are configured to process heavy sour crude, which is currently in short supply and geopolitically encumbered. This growing energy crisis is depleting the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve and driving up refined product prices relative to crude. As this supply crunch impacts public psychology and disrupts transport, agriculture, and mining, the resulting loss of economic confidence is expected to drive investors toward gold and other tangible assets.

Key points
  • The real danger in the energy market is not the price of crude oil itself, but a critical shortage of middle distillates like diesel and jet fuel. Refineries in the US and Europe are primarily configured to process heavy sour crude from Russia and the Gulf because it offers higher product profitability, meaning they cannot easily adapt to process the abundant light sweet crude produced domestically.
  • The U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) has been halved to 319.5 million barrels, leaving only about 16 days of total crude oil consumption in the system. The SPR, which is two-thirds sour crude, is being drawn down by an average of 5 million barrels a week to artificially suppress refined product prices rather than being reserved for true national security emergencies.
  • Russia has pulled diesel from the export market, causing middle distillate inventories to crash. A 5 million barrel draw in distillates was reported for the week ending July 3rd, which is highly problematic for the real economy because road transportation, agricultural harvesters, construction, and mining machinery all run on diesel.
  • Europe is highly vulnerable to this crisis, facing a shortage of 500,000 barrels of jet fuel per day in a tourism-dependent economy. Additionally, European natural gas inventories are at a 15-year low, sitting at only 40% fill compared to the historical average of 58% needed ahead of the winter season.
  • Investors tracking the precious metals market should look beyond the flat price of crude oil and closely monitor the "321 crack spread" and refined product prices. The widening gap between crude inputs and refined product prices is a key indicator of economic stress that will ultimately trigger public panic and drive a massive rush into gold.
