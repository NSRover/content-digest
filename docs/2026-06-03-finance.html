Something Strange Is Happening Inside European Banks
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)
TLDR

European banks are aggressively buying government bonds despite the European Central Bank's likely rate hikes, indicating they are preparing for a significant economic downturn, rising credit stress, and a potential private credit bust rather than sustained inflation. This behavior suggests a preference for safety and liquidity over yield and growth, contradicting the mainstream narrative of an inflation-driven hawkish central bank.

Key points
  • European banks are buying government bonds in huge amounts, which is counterintuitive given the expectation of ECB rate hikes due to elevated oil prices and inflation.
  • Banks are not positioning for inflation but rather for future economic weakness, rising credit stress, liquidity pressure, and a potential private credit bust spreading through Europe's shadow banking system.
  • The European economy was already fragile before the energy shock, with signs of contraction in France, falling household spending, declining consumer confidence, and slumping business activity across the Eurozone.
  • The energy shock is acting like a tax on an already struggling economy, reducing consumer spending and squeezing business margins, leading to shrinking volumes, delayed investment, and potential layoffs, rather than creating a durable inflation boom.
  • Forward rate markets (Euribor futures) are also "frowning," pricing in higher short-term rates in the near term due to ECB hawkishness, but then lower rates further into the future, indicating a belief that economic weakness will ultimately force rates down.
  • The private credit system in Europe is showing signs of liquidity pressure, with investment fund deposits falling and bank lending to non-bank financial institutions rising, suggesting that shadow banks are drawing on regulated bank support amidst outflows.
  • These pressures reinforce each other: the energy shock weakens the economy, which hurts corporate cash flows, increases private credit stress, leads to tighter lending, and forces banks to become more defensive, creating a cycle of economic deterioration.
Takeaway

For a retail European investor with a long-term mindset, the video suggests a cautious approach. The aggressive bond buying by European banks, coupled with the "frowning" forward rate curves, indicates a strong expectation of future economic weakness and credit stress, rather than sustained inflation. This implies that while the ECB might hike rates in the short term, the long-term outlook is for a downturn, potentially leading to lower rates later. Therefore, prioritizing safety and liquidity might be prudent.

For a short-term trade mindset, the video highlights a potential policy error by the ECB. The market is betting against the ECB's hawkish stance in the long run. If the "frown" in Euribor futures deepens, or if the curve fully inverts, it could signal an impending recession and potential rate cuts in the near term, which could present trading opportunities in bond markets or other assets sensitive to economic downturns. However, the immediate hawkishness of the ECB could still lead to short-term volatility.

  • There is growing liquidity pressure in Europe's shadow banking system, characterized as "stage two" of a private credit bust.   • Asset bubbles are driven by three forces: the strength of the narrative, liquidity, and momentum. All three are currently strong for AI and technology stocks.

  • Historically, the Federal Reserve's tightening of liquidity by raising interest rates has preceded the bursting of asset bubbles, even when the underlying technological narrative remained strong.

  • Historically, the Dow gold ratio has seen significant lows, indicating periods when gold strongly outperformed stocks, and the current trend suggests gold will again outperform the stock market.
  • A "meltup" or "crackup boom" is anticipated, where stock market prices continue to rise in fiat currency terms, but their value in real terms (gold) will decline due to hyperinflation or currency collapse.
  • Central banks, particularly China, are accumulating gold, indicating an awareness of impending currency issues, while Western central bankers are seen as underestimating gold's importance.
  • Gold and silver mining stocks are considered significantly undervalued compared to both the general stock market and the underlying physical commodities.
