Why Is The Military Divorce
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)fallback
TLDR

The divorce rate in elite military units like the Navy SEALs exceeds 100% because members frequently remarry and divorce again, driven by extreme deployment demands, youth, and a lack of work-life balance. Service members often prioritize their military commitment over their relationships, sacrificing their marriages and the people they love.

Key points
  • The divorce rate in the SEAL Teams is exceptionally high, practically exceeding 100% because it is common for members to get divorced, remarry, and then get divorced again. Having multiple marriages and divorces is considered totally normal within this community.
  • Many service members enter the military at a young age, typically between 18 and 22, and quickly marry high school sweethearts or partners they met in college. These young spouses are then relocated to unfamiliar areas without any existing personal support systems.
  • Prolonged deployments isolate spouses for up to a year at a time, and when the service member returns, they are often emotionally distant and disconnected. This cycle repeats over multiple deployments, placing immense strain on the relationship.
  • When forced to choose between their military career and their marriage, service members consistently prioritize their commitment to their unit and career over their partner, leading to inevitable relationship breakdowns.
  • The intense focus required for elite military service results in a severe lack of life balance, causing individuals to sacrifice the people they love most—a choice that serves as a major source of personal regret later in life.
Takeaway
  • Recognize the cost of extreme commitment: Achieving elite status in a highly demanding field often requires a level of singular focus that makes maintaining a healthy personal life nearly impossible.
  • The illusion of "having it all": True balance is difficult to maintain when an organization or career demands total devotion. If you do not consciously manage your priorities, your career will default to sacrificing your personal relationships.
  • Evaluate what you are willing to sacrifice: To avoid long-term regret, actively decide where your boundaries lie rather than letting a demanding career path make those sacrifices on your behalf.

Guest

Ezra Klein is a prominent journalist, political commentator, and podcast host. He is the co-author of the book *Abundance* and is known for his deep analysis of American politics, media dynamics, and policy.

TLDR

The modern political landscape is being deeply warped by the attention economy and algorithmic social media, which incentivize extreme, performative behavior over constructive governance. To navigate this and other systemic challenges like the rise of AI, individuals and leaders must cultivate intentional attention habits, prioritize human-centric virtues, and focus on real-world capacity building rather than online posturing.

Key points
  • Algorithmic media acts as a "tragedy of the commons" for collective attention, forcing political actors to escalate their rhetoric to remain visible. This constant drive for viral engagement degrades public discourse and pushes political parties into extreme, non-representative positions that alienate the median voter.
  • The Democratic Party is experiencing an internal debate between its populist and liberal wings, highlighted by the concept of "abundance" or a "liberalism that builds." This framework argues that progressive governance in blue states has become paralyzed by its own bureaucratic rules, making it too difficult to build housing and clean energy infrastructure.
  • While the political right has collapsed its standards of purity down to personal loyalty to a single leader, the left struggles with a fragmented, programmatic purity test. This makes it difficult for the left to welcome diverse candidates who can win in culturally conservative or working-class regions.
  • The debate around AI safety has been overly focused on speculative, long-term existential risks at the expense of regulating present-day harms. Effective regulation requires building state capacity and hands-on experience with current systems, rather than debating hypothetical future scenarios.
  • Modern society is suffering from "thought entropy" and the illusion of productivity driven by constant digital stimulation. To counter this, individuals must intentionally cultivate sustained attention through offline practices like reading physical books, which serve as a scaffold for deep, independent thinking.
  • The cultural left has historically alienated young men by rejecting individual self-improvement frameworks in favor of purely structural explanations. A healthy society requires balancing structural critiques with a robust, virtue-based model of self-mastery and personal agency.
Takeaway

To maintain intellectual independence in a highly digitized world, you must actively protect your "backstage" and resist outsourcing your attention to algorithms. True productivity and creativity do not come from constant information consumption, but from creating cognitive voids—such as walking without distraction or reading physical books—that allow for deep synthesis. Furthermore, recognizing that media platforms incentivize extreme "posting" rather than practical "politics" allows you to step back from unproductive political anger and focus on tangible, real-world progress.

Guest

Dr. Rachel Rubin is a board-certified urologist and sexual medicine specialist, and one of only 80 doctors in the US trained to treat both male and female sexual health. She is the founder of Rachel Rubin MD and a Director-at-Large of the International Society for the Study of Women's Sexual Health. She played a pivotal role in healthcare policy by helping remove the FDA's boxed warning on menopausal hormone therapy.

TLDR

A systemic lack of medical education regarding female anatomy and hormones has created a "hidden hormone crisis" that leaves women suffering from preventable sexual pain, low libido, and chronic health issues. By understanding the biological drivers of sexual health—such as the role of testosterone and the safety of modern hormone replacement therapy—individuals can advocate for better care and improve their quality of life.

Key points
  • The medical system frequently fails women because basic female sexual anatomy and hormonal health are often omitted from medical school curricula. For example, the word "clitoris" is not included in the standard training checklist for OB/GYNs, leading to widespread misdiagnosis of physical issues as purely psychological.
  • Menopause and perimenopause are whole-body events, not just reproductive ones. The decline in estrogen and testosterone affects the brain, heart, bones, and bladder, yet only a tiny fraction of women who could benefit from hormone therapy actually receive prescriptions due to outdated fears and misinformation.
  • Testosterone is a vital hormone for women, not just men, and its levels begin to drop precipitously in a woman's 30s. This decline can lead to reduced libido, delayed orgasm, and changes in body image, yet it is rarely discussed or measured in standard clinical settings.
  • Many common medications, including birth control pills, antidepressants, and GLP-1 weight-loss drugs, can have significant side effects on sexual function. Birth control can lower testosterone levels, while antidepressants may delay orgasm, necessitating informed consent and shared decision-making between patients and doctors.
  • The "orgasm gap" is largely driven by a lack of anatomical education. While most women require clitoral stimulation to reach orgasm, many believe they are "broken" because they do not orgasm through penetration alone—a misconception reinforced by the unrealistic portrayals of sex in pornography.
  • Chronic conditions like urinary tract infections (UTIs) in older women are often linked to the genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM). Low-dose vaginal hormones can restore the vaginal microbiome and prevent life-threatening infections, yet these treatments remain underutilized.
Takeaway

To navigate a medical system that often dismisses women's health, you must become your own advocate by understanding the biological "checking and savings" accounts of your body. Recognize that "not feeling like yourself" (NFLM) often has a physiological root, such as hormonal fluctuations or pelvic floor tension, rather than being a personal or relationship failure. Shift your mental model of sex from a spontaneous, penetration-focused event to one that prioritizes communication, curiosity, and the specific anatomical needs of your partner. By reframing sexual challenges as "us against the problem" rather than "me against you," couples can use education to remove the shame and ego that typically block intimacy.
