Is AI Exploiting OF Creators?

TLDR

Companies are reportedly offering OnlyFans performers payouts for temporary control of their accounts, then using AI to generate more extreme content under their identity, leading to significant revenue for the company and potential reputational damage for the creators.

Key points
  • Companies are approaching OnlyFans performers with existing followings, offering a payout in exchange for control of their accounts for a period, such as six months.
  • During this period, the companies use AI to create and post content that is more extreme or "wilder" than what the actual performer typically produces.
  • This AI-generated content reportedly generates a "whole crazy amount of revenue" for the company.
  • When the performer regains control of their account, they are faced with the consequences of the AI-generated content, which can include a changed perception of their brand or expectations from their audience based on the more extreme material.
Takeaway

This situation highlights how AI can be used to exploit individuals by leveraging their established identity and audience to generate content they didn't create, potentially damaging their reputation and career. It's a stark reminder to be extremely cautious about granting third parties control over one's online presence, especially when financial incentives are involved, as the long-term consequences of AI-generated content under one's name can be severe and difficult to undo.

The Psychology Of Guilt
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)
TLDR

The amount of guilt a person feels is often proportional to their perceived likelihood of being caught for the action they feel guilty about, rather than solely the severity of the transgression.

Key points
  • The intensity of guilt is strongly influenced by the perceived chance of being discovered for a misdeed.
  • If a person believes they cannot be caught, their level of guilt for an action tends to be significantly lower.
  • While the severity of the action plays a role, it interacts with the perceived likelihood of getting caught to determine the overall feeling of guilt.
  • Even minor transgressions can induce significant guilt if the probability of being caught is high, while severe actions might cause less guilt if the chance of discovery is nil.
Takeaway

This insight challenges the common assumption that guilt is purely a function of one's conscience or the moral weight of an action. Understanding that the perceived risk of exposure heavily influences guilt can help a person reflect on their own feelings of guilt more critically. It suggests that some guilt might be more about social consequences or self-preservation than a deep moral reckoning, offering a reframe for understanding personal emotional responses and potentially the motivations behind others' actions.
