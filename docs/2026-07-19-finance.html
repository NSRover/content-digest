The Fed Hasn’t Done This in Over 40 Years!! Here’s What You Must Know
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh has reintroduced money supply data (M2) into a key policy report for the first time in decades, jokingly calling it an "Easter egg." This shift represents a minor admission that the Fed's long-standing practice of ignoring actual money in favor of interest rate manipulation has left officials blind to real monetary conditions. However, M2 is a domestic aggregate that fails to capture the vast, offshore, collateral-based Eurodollar system that actually drives the global economy.

Key points
  • For decades, the Federal Reserve's policy framework has excluded actual money, relying instead on econometric models focused on interest rates, unemployment, and GDP. This monetary blindness left the Fed unable to see or prevent the 2008 financial crisis.
  • The Fed abandoned monetary targeting in 1982 because the monetary system evolved beyond its ability to measure. The rise of the Eurodollar system—a global, ledger-based network of offshore bank liabilities, repos, and derivatives—escaped the Fed's direct jurisdiction and statistical tracking.
  • By targeting the federal funds rate instead of money, the Fed began manipulating a single market rate and assuming it represented broader financial conditions. This policy shift disguised the fact that the Fed could no longer define or locate money.
  • Because the global monetary system cannot be observed directly, researchers must analyze market signals—such as yield curves, swap spreads, and collateral behavior—to understand how participants price risk and liquidity.
  • While adding M2 back into the Fed's policy discussions is a positive step, it is highly inadequate. M2 is a domestic retail aggregate that cannot capture offshore dollar creation, wholesale funding, collateral constraints, or the velocity of money.

I've Always Talked About Gold… Now I'm Inside the Mine.
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

This video provides a behind-the-scenes tour of Pilar Gold's mining and processing operations in Brazil, detailing the journey of gold ore from extraction to final shipment. The tour covers the mechanical crushing and grinding processes, the chemical extraction methods, and the underground mine environment. The host, who serves on the board of directors for Chancery Royalty, highlights the professional scale of these private, pre-IPO operations.

Key points
  • The processing plant utilizes a jawbreaker crusher to reduce raw ore down to approximately 60 centimeters in size at a rate of 200 tons per hour. A magnetic separator is then used to remove iron from the crushed ore, preventing it from interfering with the subsequent cyanide leaching process.
  • A semi-autonomous grinder (SAG) mill performs a massive reduction on the ore, grinding it down from 60 centimeters to 0.125 millimeters. The finely ground material is then classified by size using a hydrocyclone to determine its path through the leaching and smelting circuits.
  • Depending on the processing route, it takes approximately 24 to 70 hours to transform raw ore into a smelted gold doré bar. Once produced, the gold is stored in a secure safe before being transported via a Brinks helicopter to a depot in São Paulo.
  • Pilar Gold receives an initial payment of 90% to 95% of the agreed gold value almost immediately after the helicopter departs or within a day. The remaining balance is paid once the buyer receives the shipment and completes a second assay to verify the gold content.
  • The underground Pilar mine, which is scheduled to begin production in September, descends 280 meters deep (250 meters from the surface) with drives blasted to a height of four and a half meters. The mine was previously at risk of filling with water when power was cut to the pumps, but it has since been completely pumped out.
  • Pilar Gold operates multiple assets in the region, including the Maria Lazera underground mine located 4 kilometers from Pilar, the Caomar underground mine, and the Tres Barracos open-pit mine which is still under development. Ore from these satellite locations is transported to the central Pilar plant for processing.
