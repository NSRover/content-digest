The Biggest Gas vs. Oil Divergence in History (Here's What It Means)
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)fallback
TLDR

The historic divergence between oil and gas prices is due to record-high "crack spreads," which measure the profit margin for refining crude oil into finished fuels like gasoline and diesel. While crude oil supply has increased due to returning supply from the Hormuz closure and reduced demand from China, the world lacks sufficient refining capacity and inventories of finished fuels, keeping gasoline and diesel prices high despite lower crude costs and weak consumer demand. This bottleneck in refining and distribution means that lower crude prices are not fully translating to cheaper fuel at the pump.

Key points
  • The "crack spread," which is the difference between the price of crude oil and the wholesale value of refined products, is at record highs, indicating a significant profit margin for refiners. This explains why gasoline and diesel remain expensive even as crude oil prices have fallen.
  • Crude oil supply has increased because oil previously stranded during the Hormuz closure is returning to the market, and China's demand for imported crude has collapsed due to its deteriorating economy, leading to lower global crude prices.
  • Despite lower crude prices and weak consumer demand in the US, Europe, and China, inventories of gasoline and especially distillates (diesel and heating oil) are low, with US distillate inventories 12% below their 5-year seasonal average. This indicates an insufficient supply of finished fuels relative to demand.
  • Refineries face limitations in increasing output quickly, including maintenance needs, existing high utilization rates, and the inability to easily switch crude grades or produce more of one fuel without affecting others. Logistical bottlenecks in pipelines, terminals, and ports also contribute to supply issues.
  • China's reduced refining activity, while depressing crude prices, also means less gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel are produced and potentially available to the international market, further contributing to the scarcity of finished fuels globally.
  • For consumers to see lower pump prices, crack spreads must significantly decrease, which requires increased refinery output, inventory rebuilding, and clearing of logistical bottlenecks, or a substantial weakening of demand.

The Affordability Crisis Is Really a Currency Debasement Crisis.
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)fallback
TLDR

The affordability crisis is fundamentally a currency debasement crisis, not a temporary inflation problem, with purchasing power falling significantly over the last six years. Central banks are trapped by excessive debt and fragile bond markets, preventing them from genuinely fighting inflation through aggressive rate hikes or balance sheet reduction. Gold remains a crucial long-term insurance against this ongoing debasement of fiat currency.

Key points
  • The official CPI numbers understate the true rate of inflation; the actual loss of purchasing power is likely much higher, potentially exceeding 30% over six years, which is the root cause of the affordability crisis.
  • Central bankers are not genuinely fighting inflation but rather creating the perception of doing so while continuing to inflate the currency, as aggressive measures would collapse Wall Street and liquidity.
  • The Federal Reserve, like other central banks, is constrained by the need to maintain liquidity in fragile bond markets and manage excessive debt, making it difficult to implement effective anti-inflationary policies.
  • Producing physical gold is a complex and costly process, contrasting sharply with the ease of creating fiat currency, highlighting gold's intrinsic value as a store of wealth.
  • Gold serves as the best long-term insurance against the debasement of savings, as compounding even moderate rates of currency debasement can halve the currency's value over a decade.

TLDR

The Strait of Bab el-Mandeb is emerging as a critical oil chokepoint, with potential disruptions from Houthi attacks threatening to significantly raise oil prices and insurance costs, especially for Saudi crude. This comes after a period where the Strait of Hormuz was effectively closed, not by Iran, but through a strategic manipulation of the insurance industry by the US, aimed at asserting energy and AI dominance over China. The ongoing instability highlights the vulnerability of global oil supply chains and the need for new strategies like building strategic petroleum reserves in consumer nations.

Key points
  • The Strait of Bab el-Mandeb is a new, under-watched oil chokepoint, through which about 6 million barrels of oil pass daily. Houthi attacks, potentially influenced by Iranian hardliners, could disrupt this flow, leading to higher oil prices and insurance costs, particularly impacting Saudi oil exports.
  • The previous closure of the Strait of Hormuz was a strategic move by the US, not Iran, using EU insurance regulations to effectively trap tankers and disrupt oil flow. This was part of a broader US strategy to achieve energy and AI dominance over China, impacting Asian economies through helium and energy shortages.
  • Iranian hardliners in the IRGC have benefited greatly from sanctions and have actively worked to derail negotiations, acting like a cartel to maintain their control and wealth. Their influence extends to various armed groups internationally, enabling them to cause widespread disruption.
  • Global oil inventory declines have been largely due to Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) releases, primarily by the US and Japan, rather than commercial inventory drawdowns. These SPR releases, often structured as loans, have helped stabilize product prices, especially diesel, and will likely be refilled at lower prices, not causing significant future price increases.
  • The real shortages are in refined petroleum products like diesel and gasoline, not crude oil, as refining capacity is maxed out. High product prices are already leading to demand destruction, a permanent shift away from fossil fuels, rather than just a temporary decline.
  • A strategic alliance between OPEC+ and consuming nations, involving investment in massive strategic petroleum reserves in market locations like India, is proposed as a cost-effective solution to mitigate chokepoint risks and ensure supply security.
  • The "ghost of Hormuz" will persist, making chokepoints permanent targets for disruption. This will likely lead countries to adopt a "Chinese model" of linking energy sources to national security, promoting domestic energy production, and increasing investment in natural gas, LNG, and coal to meet growing energy demands from data centers and AI.

How America Built A Durable Dollar Empire | Mary Bridges
gemini-flash (gemini-2.5-flash)fallback
TLDR

The US dollar's rise to global dominance was a messy, improvised process driven by both profit-seeking private banks and the strategic interests of the US government. This expansion, initially a copycat of the British system, relied heavily on building an information infrastructure and was significantly bolstered by the creation of the Federal Reserve and its support for instruments like bankers' acceptances. The story highlights the deep entanglement of public and private sectors in establishing America's financial empire.

Key points
  • Before 1898, the US dollar was a second-rate currency, with American international trade largely dependent on the British pound and London-based financial institutions. This reliance created limitations for US traders and the government, leading to a desire for independent financial infrastructure.
  • The International Banking Corporation (IBC), a uniquely chartered bank, pioneered American multinational branch banking, often operating at a loss initially. Its primary motivation was to attract US corporate clients by offering an international network and the prestige associated with it, rather than immediate foreign banking profits.
  • The expansion of US banking overseas, particularly through institutions like IBC and later National City Bank, was not a grand strategic plot but a messy, often improvised process driven by competing interests and requiring political opportunities and government support, such as colonial government deposits.
  • Information, its creation, organization, and dissemination, played a central role in building the infrastructure for American financial hegemony. Banks meticulously collected and systematized credit information about international trading partners, which became a key selling point for their services.
  • The Federal Reserve Act of 1913 was crucial for the internationalization of the dollar, as it removed prohibitions against national banks operating overseas branches and, more importantly, created a market for bankers' acceptances. The Fed's role as the primary buyer of these instruments was essential for their widespread adoption and the shift from sterling to dollar-based trade credit.
  • The relationship between the US government and private financial institutions overseas was often symbiotic, with a "revolving door" between colonial government offices and banking practices. However, tensions could arise when government policies, such as promoting self-governance in the Philippines, conflicted with the established interests of the American business community.
