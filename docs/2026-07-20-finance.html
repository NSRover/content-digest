The Oil Market and Yield Curve Just Did Something Really Strange
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

A major correlation has broken down as US Treasury yields and TIPS breakevens are falling even as West Texas Intermediate (WTI) oil prices rise back into the $80 range. This divergence suggests the bond market is no longer pricing in a prolonged rate-hiking cycle driven by oil-induced inflation, but is instead anticipating demand destruction and a weakening consumer. Consequently, the market is signaling that any rate hikes by the Federal Reserve under Kevin Warsh will likely be a "one and done" scenario followed by rate cuts.

Key points
  • The historical correlation where rising oil prices drove bond yields higher due to anticipated Federal Reserve rate hikes has broken down, with yields and TIPS breakevens now falling despite oil prices climbing.
  • Recent CPI and PPI data show no evidence of second-round inflation effects from energy prices, indicating to the market that the real risk is demand destruction rather than persistent inflation.
  • Weakening economic indicators, such as a drop in pending home sales and mortgage lending falling to its lowest level since February, suggest that consumers and businesses are running out of money and cannot absorb higher borrowing costs.
  • Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh is expected to hike rates to establish the central bank's independence and inflation-fighting credibility, but the market is pricing this as a "one and done" move that the Fed will quickly have to reverse.
  • Artificially high short-term rates set by the Fed risk further inverting the yield curve, which compresses bank margins, tightens lending standards, and threatens to trigger a credit cycle bust.
  • This economic slowdown and tightening of bank credit is a global phenomenon, as evidenced by European banks unexpectedly tightening lending standards and loading up on safe government bonds.

Why the Thucydides Trap has EVERYTHING to do with Markets...
gemini-3-5-flash (gemini-3.5-flash)
TLDR

The famous Thucydides quote, "the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must," is widely misunderstood as a defense of raw power. According to scholar Jonathan Kirshner, Thucydides actually used the Melian Dialogue to show how Athens's arrogance and brutality toward a neutral island led to its own eventual demise in Sicily. This historical lesson on hubris and the consequences of power politics is highly relevant to modern financial markets as the global economy shifts from cooperation to de-globalization.

Key points
  • The popular interpretation of the Thucydides Trap, famously detailed in Graham Allison's book *Destined for War*, focuses on the inevitable conflict between a rising power like China and an established power like the United States. However, scholar Jonathan Kirshner argues that Thucydides's true warning was not about the war itself, but about how an established or rising power becomes corrupted by its own success and hubris.
  • The Melian Dialogue details Athens's ultimatum to the neutral island of Milos to surrender or be destroyed. When Milos refused, Athens slaughtered the men, enslaved the women and children, and repopulated the island. Thucydides focused heavily on this minor, strategically insignificant event to highlight the moral decay and reckless arrogance that Athens developed mid-war.
  • Athens's brutality backfired because it drove neutral parties to side with its rivals and destroyed its own alliances. Immediately after crushing Milos, Athens launched a disastrous campaign in Sicily where its fleet and army were destroyed, a catastrophe from which Athens never recovered.
  • This historical dynamic mirrors modern geopolitics, such as the United States's swift success in Venezuela followed by its more difficult military engagement in Iran. Investors must understand both sides of geopolitical arguments, as arrogance and overconfidence can lead to severe policy backfires.
  • While ancient Greek history may seem unrelated to modern financial markets, it has everything to do with them today. The global market is currently experiencing a major pendulum swing away from globalization and cooperation toward de-globalization and "America first" policies, making macroeconomics and geopolitics highly critical factors for investors.
